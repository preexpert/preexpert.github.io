Books

Brief descriptions of notable books I've read since 2021, written in ~10 minutes or less.

Value(s): Building a better world for all - Mark Carney

Mark Carney ran the Bank of Canada and Bank of England over an eventful stretch of time (GFC, Brexit, early Covid), which I figured would give him some interesting perspectives on the nature of the economy, politics, and leadership. I was very interested to hear his simple views on complex topics. In no particular order: What is the nature of money and what does the fintech revolution mean for the financial system? What's the real purpose of a commercial bank? How does he think money is created? What does he think of gold? How does he think about objective versus subjective value? What's the value of a human life? Should old people be valued differently than the young? What do central bankers know or think they know about the future? Are they aware of their limitations when communicating policy to the public? Thoughts on all of this are in the book, but I did have to dig for it a bit - I thought the structure of the book was a bit tough to navigate and I ended up reading chapters out of order. It was a worthwhile battle though and kudos to Carney for writing a tough book (and also for being a stoic and a fan of Marcus Aurelius). Apr 15 2021.

Complexity: The Emerging Science at the Edge of Order and Chaos - Mitchell Waldrop

I loved this book. I said to my wife one night "I think this is one of my favorite books ever". Oddly enough I was listening to Bill Gurley on Masters of Business with Barry Ritholtz the next day and he said it was one of his favorite books. I've been interested in complexity, the game of life, genetic algorithms, and chaos. I have sunk a lot of time into thinking about it. And here it is all laid out in a readable tale of how these scientific wanderers came together and made the Santa Fe Institute. I was also wasting time a few weeks back watching YouTube clips of glaciers breaking off into the ocean and for some reason, despite stretches of nothing happening, finding it hard to look away. I realized eventually the size of the chunks breaking off is essentially stochastic and the unpredictability means any given second something big could happen. Hence you stick around waiting for it. Then I read in this book about self organized criticality and the emergence of power laws at the edge of chaos. It's rare a book really drops so many answers into the gaps in your thinking. Clearly this whole thing was right up my alley. The book is a bit light on detail - ie. how do you actually model agent-based programs. But that type of content is tough (and potentially not that useful) to put in a book - it needs to be attempted/experienced. I may have to do some Santa Fe courses now so I can explore more. Mar 31 2021.

Money: The True Story of a Made Up Thing - Jacob Goldstein

This book takes the reader through the history of money and the bigger picture view that money changes forms over time and when it does, it is always resisted at first. This is not some over-simplified glossing over of the conventional narratives behind the history of money (inefficiency of barter, the cause of the GFC was the banks or the mortgages, etc). This is a well researched, quasi-revisionist history take on money. While reading this book I kept thinking about Tor Nerretrander's book The User illusion (see review below), which talks about how information can be more reflective of what is thrown away rather than what was kept. Goldstein delves into ancient history and modern economic theory and excises the critical parts to deliver the content in an understandable way. This is an extremely difficult task and I think he pulled it off. Lesser authors have tried and failed. Mar 14 2021.

The Fourth Turning - William Strauss & Neil Howe

I believe in cycles. They are a natural result of lag times in a system seeking equilibrium, which results in overshoot and undershoot. The only time the system is in equilibrium is when it passes through it. I believe cycles of different frequencies are happening all the time, and occassionally they reach a minimum or maximum together and it makes for a unique event (micro cycles combine to form macro cycles). The problem I have is a macro cycle thesis is hard to disprove. For example I think of Carlota Perez's Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital - there are some very cool conceptual maps of when technology is installed and deployed, but the times are so vast that you can basically pick your events to fit the framework or adapt it. You're basically trying to use patterns to fit a historical set of narratives. I've also noticed, the more the author and the reader know about a given period - typically the most recent period - the harder it is to make the story work (because the reader and author are more aware of the nuances). So the Fourth Turning is fun (in the way reading a horoscope is fun), but I'll be honest - I flipped through most of it. If I tried to read certain sections my brain would wander. I think it's because I don't believe most of the content. I'm also not a fan of such a US-centric cycle view when we are dealing with a global economy and global technologies (internet, automation, etc). Now I have to give the Fourth Turning credit - it nailed the predictions. It really is something to look at in retrospect. Terrorist attacks, a financial crisis, a pandemic, and social and political unrest was not the most likely combination! And maybe the thesis is even "right" that darkness continues until the next turning. But does this mean the author's nailed it? Or is it survivorship bias (we're only reading this book because it made successful predictions - I haven't even heard of the cycle books that didn't work out). Mar 2 2021.

User Illusion - Tor Norretranders

This was a great read about consciousness. It covered ground I've already spent a fair bit of time on: entropy (macro and microstates), information, paradoxes, etc. I was familiar with some of the stuff about the nature of the brain (system 1 and 2, split brain experiments, the way our consciousness makes things up). But there was a lot of content that was new (consciousness processing rates, response times, and the specific experiments behind each thesis). The book was probably a bit too long and I found some parts repetitive, but the author covers a lot of complicated ground and I thought he made the material very digestible. He also presented areas of debate so the reader is aware of the controversies and open questions. I'm going to have to go back to this one at some point. Areas I need to spend time to further understand are the interaction of blackholes and information theory, how consciousness came to humankind, the collapse of communism as a function of information, how earth exports disorder? Feb 23 2021.

Fed Up - Danielle DiMartino Booth

This was a quick and easy read - it's mostly a memoir of Danielle's time at the Dallas Fed. While it is clear that the writer disagrees with the Fed's understanding and control of the US (and global) economy, I think that's pretty well understood already. I read "The Man Who Knew" a few years ago and it's got a part of it where the Maestro and the Board get into a debate about what causes inflation and it becomes clear no one really knows what they're talking about. So this has been understood for decades. It's not clear if there's any better way to do it. Would it have been useful to tighten into the GFC? Probably not. What about letting all the banks fail? What about letting none of them fail? Does the Fed actually have any real power or are they doomed to steer things where they were going anyways? There are no answers in this book, but I'm not sure there are anywhere. Feb 10 2021.

Beyond diversification - Sebastian Page

I heard an interview with Sebastian and was intrigued enough to get his book. It's largely a collection of his views on the big market debates - how to forecast risk, reward, and construct a portfolio. On return forecasting, you can use a risk-pricing approach or a valuation approach. His view is that CAPM is flawed (gaussian, rational investor, valuation agnostic, betas not forward looking) but more useful than investors think. He quotes Markowitz "it's like studying the motion of objects on earth under the assumption that the earth has no air". Simple back of the envelope approaches can be useful too. Take the E/P ratio and add the 10yr breakeven. Bonds are easier though - take the yield to maturity and leave it at that! He's also got book recommendations - "Capital Ideas" by Bernstein and "Capital Ideas Evolving". Adding to my list... Feb 8 2021.

The Great Rupture - Viktor Shvets

Viktor has a unique big picture perspective on the world having lived in Russia, China and the West. He is very well read and tries to take lessons of past empires to understand the conditions that created the modern world, and tries to make some projections of what lies ahead. That is a difficult task - he admits that the changes in both technology and financialization are unprecedented and happening much faster than past transitions. The outlook is not good, in short. Massive change, displacement of jobs, potential for conflict, and transition from a capitalist economy to communism. But it doesn't have to be all doom and gloom. There will be a bright future beyond the transition. Jan 30 2021.

Star Principle & 80/20 principle - Richard Koch

I heard Tim Ferriss interview Richard who talked about the 80/20 principle and the Star principle so I picked up the books. The 80/20 principle or Pareto principle is quite well known but Richard reminds you to focus on the parts of your life that add value. It is a really glutinous efficient way to run your life and I can see why a "Four Hour Work Week" Ferriss would like it. Lots of aspirational advice. The Star Principle is about investing in growth companies. It's not typically my game but in a complicated world there is something to be said for its simplicity - buy the industry leaders in fast growing sectors. This was written in 2010 - suffice it to say if you followed his advice up until now you would have smashed it out of the park. I'm not sure it would do that well from here, however. Jan 8 2021
